## **Blue Machine Write-up**

### **1. Machine Information**

* **Name:** `Blue`
* **IP Address:** `10.129.39.62`
* **Difficulty:** `Easy`
* **OS:** `Windows`

---

## **2. Enumeration**

### **Initial Nmap Scan**

```bash
nmap -sS -sV -sC -Pn -T4 10.129.39.62
```

![alt_text](/assets/images/blue/image1.png "image_tooltip")

The Nmap results show that SMB-related ports are open, especially port `445`, which immediately catches our attention.

Let’s check whether anonymous SMB login is allowed.

---

### **SMB Enumeration**

![alt_text](/assets/images/blue/image2.png "image_tooltip")

Anonymous login is enabled and we can access the available shares.

We also notice that the `Share` and `Users` shares do not contain the `$` sign, meaning they are not hidden administrative shares and can be accessed normally.

After looking around the shares, nothing useful was found.

---

## **3. Vulnerability Identification**

However, from the initial Nmap scan we already know two very important things:

* The machine is running `Windows 7`
* Port `445` is publicly accessible

Only one condition is left to verify whether the machine is vulnerable to `MS17-010` (EternalBlue / WannaCry).

![alt_text](/assets/images/blue/image3.png "image_tooltip")

`SMBv1` is enabled.

This means...

![alt_text](/assets/images/blue/image4.png "image_tooltip")

The machine is vulnerable to `MS17-010`.

---

## **4. Exploitation**

We will use `Metasploit` to exploit the vulnerability and gain a reverse shell.

![alt_text](/assets/images/blue/image5.png "image_tooltip")

After configuring the exploit and payload:

![alt_text](/assets/images/blue/image6.png "image_tooltip")

We got a shell.

But not just any shell...

![alt_text](/assets/images/blue/image7.png "image_tooltip")

We obtained a `SYSTEM` shell, which is the highest privilege level on Windows systems.
